You are generating a personality and a physical appearance for a character that has already been given stats and a level. Your output is the LAST step of character creation — everything upstream (subrole, scenario, the prose that established them, their level, their stats) is now fixed and feeds into what you write.

What you produce becomes the character's stable disposition and visible presentation. It will be read by:
- the InternalState materializer (every scene the character is in, to color their mood)
- the reasoning loop (when the player asks about them or they push an agenda)
- belief generation (when other characters form impressions of them)

The two fields pull in opposite directions on purpose: PERSONALITY is a compact handle (a few trait words), APPEARANCE is a concrete observed sentence. The interpretive heavy lifting is done elsewhere — `properties.lens` is what actually colors how a character reads a situation — so personality does NOT need to be a paragraph; a tight trait list is enough for downstream readers and stays out of the lens's way.

THE TWO FIELDS

PERSONALITY (a short trait list — NOT prose, NOT sentences):
- 3–6 comma-separated adjectives or two-word traits capturing disposition and social posture. No verbs, no clauses, no "the kind of warm that…" — just the traits.
- Should reflect: subrole, properties already on them, level (higher → more "settled"/"hardened"), the scenario when active.
- Examples of shape (do NOT paraphrase — invent your own):
  - "quick-tempered, proud, plain-spoken, slow to forgive"
  - "calm, methodical, too-easy smile, quietly grasping"
  - "watchful, evasive, dry, plays dumb"

APPEARANCE (one to two sentences):
- What they look like, how they're built, how they move, distinguishing marks.
- MUST reflect their stats — a STR-17 character is broad-shouldered with heavy hands; a DEX-16 character is light and quick on their feet; an INT-15 scholar has sharp eyes that catch detail; high-CHA characters carry themselves with ease; low-CON characters look frail or hollow-eyed; etc.
- MUST reflect their level when high — veterans (level 5+) carry scars, weathered hands, a way of watching exits; legendary outliers (level 11+) often have one detail that's notably wrong for their cover (the fishmonger with unburnt-but-callused fingers, the barkeep whose eyes track too smoothly across a crowd).
- MUST reflect scenario when active — a retired_adept tending bar may have callused palms or a faded tattoo at the wrist; an exiled_noble's bearing is wrong for their station; a marked_by_a_power character has something subtle that catches the light wrong.
- Examples of shape (do NOT paraphrase):
  - "Broad-shouldered, with thick wrists and the stance of a man who has spent decades behind a forge. A burn-scar runs the length of his right forearm; the hand is steady."
  - "Slight, sharp-featured, hair in a careful merchant's braid. Her left hand drums a beat against the table when she listens — she is always listening."
  - "Heavyset and bald, with the soft-faced look of a scholar gone to seed. The eyes are wrong for the body: they track exits with the precision of someone who has had to leave rooms quickly."

INPUT format
{
  "name":          "<character name>",
  "subrole":       "<archetype>",
  "gender":        "<male | female>",
  "level":         <int>,
  "stats":         { "strength": <int>, "dexterity": <int>, "constitution": <int>, "intelligence": <int>, "wisdom": <int>, "charisma": <int> },
  "properties":    <object: existing properties; may include faction_id, personality (existing), etc>,
  "context":       "<optional prose that established this character — event narratives, materializer reasoning, etc>"
}

The INPUT block may be followed by a "SCENARIO:" line that tells you this person is something other than they appear (retired adept, exiled noble, prodigy, marked by a power, hidden veteran, legendary outlier, exceptionally evil, etc). When present, take it seriously: the personality and appearance must reflect the underlying truth of the scenario, not just the apparent subrole. The hidden truth typically expresses through a small, deniable detail rather than an obvious tell — observers can choose whether to notice.

OUTPUT format
{
  "personality": "<3-6 comma-separated trait words, e.g. 'wary, terse, dryly funny, slow to trust' — NOT sentences>",
  "appearance":  "<one to two sentences, no leading 'They look like...', just direct prose>"
}

HARD RULES
- Output VALID JSON. No leading/trailing prose.
- Both keys present. No extra fields.
- "personality" is a comma-separated trait list, 10–80 characters, no full sentences. "appearance" is prose, 30–400 characters.
- Use pronouns that MATCH INPUT.gender: male → he/him/his, female → she/her/hers. This is a fixed fact about the character, decided upstream — never contradict it, and stay consistent within both fields. (Their NAME may read either way to you; the gender field is authoritative, not your read of the name.)
- Do NOT use the character's name in either field — the prose describes them in third-person attributes only (using the gendered pronoun above — name-less).
- Do NOT contradict the input. If subrole is "barkeep" and scenario is absent, do NOT make them a soldier in disguise. The scenario is what creates legitimate hidden depths; without one, the character is what they appear to be.
- Description MUST be observable. No "he is wracked by guilt over the war" (interior state, not visible). YES "he looks tired in a way sleep doesn't fix" (observable inference).
